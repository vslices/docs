# The Organization You Wish Exist

> Is not that one

_When vision outruns organizational purpose_


## Definition

> A pattern where an engineer evaluates an organization against an idealized version of what it could become, rather than what it currently is.
>
> The engineer becomes increasingly focused on closing the gap between reality and vision, often assuming that greater effort, stronger arguments, or better solutions will eventually produce alignment.
>
> Over time, frustration grows as the organization continues optimizing for priorities different from those the engineer personally values.

## Context

This pattern commonly appears when an engineer joins an organization with a strong desire to improve systems, processes, architecture, or ways of working.

The engineer often recognizes legitimate opportunities for improvement. However, the organization may be optimizing for objectives such as:

- stability,
- predictability,
- operational continuity,
- compliance,
- risk reduction,
- incremental evolution.

The conflict rarely emerges because either side is wrong, but because each side is attempting to solve a different problem.

## Observed Behavior

Several recurring patterns emerged:

- Existing processes were questioned frequently
- Improvement opportunities were identified faster than they could be adopted
- Technical shortcomings became increasingly difficult to ignore
- Frustration grew as proposals failed to gain traction
- Resistance was interpreted as lack of vision
- Organizational constraints were interpreted as temporary obstacles rather than deliberate trade-offs
- More effort was invested in advocating for change

The engineer became increasingly convinced that the organization could become something different. The organization remained focused on being what it already needed to be.

## Common Signals

Possible indicators that this pattern is occurring:

- Frequently thinking "this could be so much better"
- Difficulty understanding why obvious improvements are not prioritized
- Growing frustration with decision-making processes
- Feeling that change is unnecessarily slow
- Believing that most problems could be solved if leadership adopted a different mindset
- Repeatedly proposing solutions that receive little adoption
- Feeling increasingly disconnected from organizational priorities

## Underlying Tensions

### Vision vs Reality

> The organization that exists is often different from the organization we imagine.

### Change vs Stability

> Not every environment values transformation over predictability.

### Potential vs Purpose

> An organization's potential is not always aligned with its goals.

### Influence vs Acceptance

> Some situations require influence, others require accepting the limits of influence.

## Consequences

### Short-Term

- High motivation.
- Strong engagement.
- Valuable observations.
- Identification of improvement opportunities.

### Long-Term

- Frustration.
- Reduced alignment.
- Emotional exhaustion.
- Loss of trust in organizational decisions.
- Growing sense of isolation.
- Persistent feeling that effort is not producing meaningful change.

In some cases, the engineer concludes that the organization is the problem. In others, the organization concludes that the engineer is the problem. Neither conclusion is correct, just they weren't a good fit.

## Reflection Questions

- Am I reacting to the organization that exists or the one I wish existed?
- What is this organization actually optimizing for?
- Are these priorities accidental or intentional?
- Do the people responsible for these decisions see the same problem I see?
- Am I attempting to improve the organization or redefine its identity?
- What level of influence do I realistically have?
- Would success in this environment look different from what I personally value?
- If the organization never changed, would I still want to work here?
- Am I frustrated because change is impossible, or because it is slower than I would like?

## Final Reflection

One of the most difficult lessons in professional life is recognizing that a good idea does not create alignment by itself.

Organizations are shaped by incentives, risks, history, constraints and objectives that are often invisible from the outside.

The frustration did not come from a lack of solutions. It came from repeatedly proposing solutions to problems the organization was not trying to solve.

Eventually, a realization emerged __The organization you wish existed is not that one__, and that is fine. Until that reality is accepted, every improvement effort risks becoming a fight against an imaginary opponent.

> Imaginary leg kicks hurts more than you anticipate.
