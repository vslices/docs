# Candles Bring Light Even In The Sun

## Definition

> A pattern where an engineer struggles to recognize the value of their own contributions because the work feels natural, intuitive or effortless compared to the effort it appears to require from others.
>
> Recognition is often questioned not because of a lack of competence, but because the engineer associates value with effort rather than outcomes.
>
> Over time, this can create a constant pressure to justify success through increasing difficulty, responsibility or sacrifice.

## Context

This pattern commonly appears in engineers who develop expertise unusually early or possess natural strengths that allow them to solve problems faster than their peers.

The engineer often receives recognition, trust and responsibilities that appear disproportionate to the effort required to achieve the results.

- While others observe competence, the engineer observes familiarity.
- While others see skill, the engineer sees something that feels obvious.

This creates an uncomfortable question:

> If this feels easy, why does everyone think it is valuable?

## Observed Behavior

Several recurring patterns emerged:

- Achievements were frequently minimized internally.
- Recognition felt disproportionate to the effort invested.
- Difficult problems appeared easier than expected.
- Success was attributed to luck, circumstance or simplicity.
- Increasingly difficult challenges were pursued to justify recognition.
- Personal value became associated with effort rather than outcomes.
- The engineer struggled to understand why others found certain tasks difficult.

The engineer did not doubt the results, he doubted whether those results should count.

## Common Signals

Possible indicators that this pattern is occurring:

- "It wasn't that difficult."
- "Anyone could have done this."
- "I don't understand why everyone is impressed."
- Feeling uncomfortable receiving praise.
- Seeking increasingly difficult challenges to feel legitimate.
- Measuring value primarily through effort.
- Believing that meaningful work must be difficult work.
- Assuming that what feels natural must therefore be unremarkable.

## Underlying Tensions

### Effort vs Value

> Valuable work is not always difficult work.

### Talent vs Earned Recognition

> Some contributions create value regardless of how natural they feel.

### Humility vs Self-Denial

> Humility recognizes strengths. Self-denial rejects them.

### Results vs Suffering

> The market rewards outcomes. Not the amount of pain required to produce them.

## Consequences

### Short-Term

- Strong drive for self-improvement.
- Consistent pursuit of mastery.
- Willingness to accept difficult challenges.

### Long-Term

- Difficulty accepting recognition.
- Chronic dissatisfaction with achievements.
- Overcommitment.
- Unrealistic personal expectations.
- Constant need to prove worth.
- Increased risk of burnout.

Ironically, the engineer often becomes respected precisely because of the abilities they struggle to acknowledge.

## Reflection Questions

- Am I measuring value through effort or outcomes?
- Would I value this contribution if someone else had made it?
- Am I dismissing a strength simply because it feels natural?
- What evidence suggests that this work was valuable?
- Do I believe recognition must be earned through suffering?
- Am I pursuing growth or attempting to justify my existence?
- What would happen if I simply accepted that I am good at this?

## Final Reflection

One of the most difficult lessons in professional life is realizing that effort and value are related, but not equivalent.

Some people spend years developing abilities that eventually make difficult work appear effortless. Others are fortunate enough to discover certain abilities earlier. Neither diminishes the value created.

> A team may spend months building a house.
> A more experienced team may build a mansion in the same amount of time.
> A particularly talented individual may build a house with surprising ease.

For years, I believed that if building a house felt easy, the house could not be worth much. 

But the problem wasn't the house, it was that I had never noticed I was capable of building mansions.

The candle does not stop bringing light simply because the sun is already shining. Its contribution may appear small in comparison. But light remains real.

> A candle that denies its own light does not become humble.
>
> It merely leaves part of the world in darkness or homeless.
