# Self-Immolation Through Overcommitment

_When personal sacrifice is used to compensate for misalignment_

## Definition

> A pattern where an engineer repeatedly accepts commitments beyond sustainable capacity in an attempt to:
> - Help a team member (More complex that it seems)
> - Reduce pressure from stakeholders 
> - Accelerate outcomes
> - To justify or prove their own value
> - To feel indispensable
> 
> This behavior is often motivated by:
> - Deep empathy and low disconfort tolerance
> - Expanded sense of responsability
> - Genuine desire to contribute
> 
> Over time, the accumulated commitments become impossible to sustain, resulting in:
> - Exhaustion and burnout 
> - Reduced predictability
> - Delayed deliveries (further than an early renegotiation would have)

## Context

This pattern was observed within a large and highly structured organization.

The engineer involved was accustomed to environments where challenging assumptions, proposing improvements, and driving change were actively encouraged. However, the organization prioritized:

- predictability
- process adherence
- risk reduction
- controlled evolution

The mismatch was subtle and difficult to recognize. So, the engineer interpreted organizational friction as a signal that more effort was required.

In reality, the organization was not asking for more effort, but different behavior.

## Observed behavior

Several recurring patterns emerged:

- Aggressive deadlines were accepted repeatedly
- Estimates became increasingly optimistic
- Commitments were made before sufficient information was available
- Personal or team capacity was overestimated
- Technical concerns were postponed in favor of delivery promises
- Additional work was absorbed to protect project stakeholders from external pressure

The engineer believed that greater effort would eventually close the gap. But as the exhaustion and burnout grows, the gap follow along.

## Common Signals

Possible indicators that this pattern is occurring:

- Frequent re-estimation of the same work
- Feeling personally responsible for project outcomes beyond one's role
- Difficulty declining commitments
- Increasing work hours without corresponding delivery improvements
- Persistent feeling that "one more push" will solve the problem
- Repeated promises to compensate for previous delays
- Feeling guilty when communicating realistic timelines
- Feeling that saying "no" is equivalent to abandoning the team

## Underlying Tension

### Empathy vs Sustainability

> The desire to help others can exceed personal capacity.

### Commitment vs Capacity

> Commitments become disconnected from observable delivery capability.

### Effort vs Alignment

> Additional effort is used to compensate for organizational misalignment.

## Consequences

### Short-Term
- Temporary perception of commitment
- Increased stakeholder confidence
- Reduced immediate conflict

### Long-Term
- Burnout
- Reduced delivery predictability
- Loss of confidence in estimates
- Increased project delays
- Accumulated frustration
- Personal exhaustion

Ironically, the attempt to reduce pressure often creates more pressure later.

## Reflection Questions

- Am I committing based on evidence or hope?
- What data supports this estimate?
- Who benefits from this commitment?
- What would happen if I communicated the actual timeline?
- Am I solving a delivery problem or reacting to pressure?
- Am I trying to help the team, or trying to protect them from realities they need to see?
  - Would the team be better served by transparency than sacrifice?
- Is this a capacity problem, or an alignment problem?
- What part of this situation is actually under my control?

## Final Reflection

The most painful lesson from this experience was not the fire. It was realizing that they wanted a campfire, not a personfire:

- The objective was never to deliver faster, the objective was to reduce pressure on people who were already under pressure.
- The engineer attempted to solve an organizational problem through personal sacrifice. At the end of the day, the sacrifice was real, but the problem remained.

More effort can compensate for many things. But most of the time, misalignment is not one of them.
