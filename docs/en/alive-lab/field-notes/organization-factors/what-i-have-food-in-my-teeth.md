# What Do You Mean I Have Food Between My Teeth?

> Why did nobody tell me sooner?!

*When uncomfortable feedback arrives too late*

## Synthetic Field Note

This note was born from an interpretive error during the translation of [Wait... Is That Child Drinking Vodka?](wait-is-that-child-drinking-vodka.md).

It was preserved as a field note under the label "synthetic" because it captures a real and useful observation, even though it did not emerge from direct experience.

It should be read as a complementary observation within Alive Lab.

## Definition

> A pattern where an organization, team, or person ignores an obvious problematic signal for too long because the signal feels too uncomfortable, absurd, embarrassing, or disruptive to confront immediately.
>
> When the problem is finally acknowledged, the reaction is often surprise or disbelief, even though the evidence had been present much earlier.

## Context

This pattern appears when a visible problem exists for a considerable amount of time, but the surrounding environment normalizes it, minimizes it, or avoids it.

At first, the signal may seem too strange to accept:

* "That cannot be happening."
* "Surely it is not that serious."
* "Maybe we are just interpreting it wrong."
* "We do not want to overreact."

Discomfort delays the response. It postpones it until the situation becomes too obvious to deny.

## Observed Behavior

Several recurring patterns were observed:

* Warning signals were seen early but treated as noise.
* Social discomfort outweighed the willingness to intervene.
* The reaction only arrived after the problem became undeniable.
* People assumed someone else had already intervened.
* The problem kept growing while everyone waited for a clearer signal.
* When action finally happened, the response felt late or exaggerated.

The strangeness of the problem made people doubt themselves even when the evidence was right in front of them.

## Common Signals

Possible indicators that this pattern is occurring:

* Feeling that something is wrong, but waiting for "more evidence."
* Seeing a worrying signal and not mentioning it to avoid discomfort.
* Assuming someone else has already handled the problem.
* Normalizing something only because it has been happening for a while.
* Nervously laughing at a situation that actually requires intervention.
* Feeling relief when someone else finally says out loud what everyone was seeing.
* Confusing social avoidance with prudence.

## Underlying Tensions

### Discomfort vs Intervention

> Uncomfortable signals are often precisely the ones that require early action.

### Denial vs Evidence

> The more obvious a problem is, the easier it can become to keep avoiding it for social reasons.

### Clarity vs Embarrassment

> Naming something uncomfortable can be more useful than protecting the group's discomfort.

### Time vs Cost

> Ignored problems often become more expensive before they are acknowledged.

## Consequences

### Short-Term

* Conflict avoidance.
* Less immediate discomfort.
* Temporary sense of normality.

### Long-Term

* Larger problems.
* Loss of trust.
* Late reaction.
* Higher cost of correction.
* Accumulated frustration.
* Retrospective sense of obviousness.

Ironically, the problem often seems ridiculous only after it has already caused harm.

## Engineering Impact

* Technical, organizational, or human risk signals may be visible long before someone acts.
* Teams may postpone important conversations because the topic feels too uncomfortable or politically risky.
* Defects, bad habits, or misalignments may become normalized until they become part of the environment.
* Lack of early intervention increases the cost of correction.
* People may confuse delay with caution and caution with wisdom.
* The organization may lose internal credibility when evidence is repeatedly ignored.
* Problems escalate because nobody wants to be the first person to say the obvious.
* Late response often feels dramatic because it arrives after a long period of silent denial.
* Culture can teach people to doubt their own perception.
* Engineering work becomes harder when reality has to become scandalous before it is addressed.

When an uncomfortable signal is ignored for too long, the problem stops being only the initial problem. It also becomes a problem of perception, trust, and courage to name it.

## Possible Interventions

* Name the uncomfortable signal before it grows further.
* Distinguish real prudence from social avoidance.
* Ask what evidence would already be enough to act.
* Treat discomfort as a reason to look more closely, not less.
* Listen to the person seeing the problem before listening to the comfort of the group.
* Make risks visible even when the conversation feels uncomfortable.
* Reduce the time between observation and acknowledgement.
* Avoid waiting for unanimity before intervening.
* Ask what harm may occur while everyone waits for a better signal.
* Use clear language instead of euphemisms when the problem is already visible.
* Accept that early intervention often looks exaggerated right before it becomes clearly correct.

A useful intervention is to ask: "Are we being prudent, or are we just avoiding the uncomfortable moment of admitting what we already know?"

That question can cut through inertia before the situation gets worse.

## Reflection Questions

* What signal am I seeing but not naming?
* Am I waiting for more evidence, or am I avoiding discomfort?
* Who has already seen the problem and still has not said it?
* What harm could happen while we keep waiting?
* Am I confusing silence with stability?
* What would be more costly: speaking now or speaking later?
* What am I pretending not to understand?

## Final Reflection

One of the most human mistakes in organizations is waiting too long to acknowledge the obvious when the obvious is uncomfortable.

The problem rarely disappears because it is ignored. It only becomes stranger, more expensive, and harder to explain.

> If it makes you think "wait, do I have food between my teeth?" o "wait, do that guy have food between their teeth?", it is probably not the time to keep pretending nobody can see it.
