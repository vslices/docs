# Knowledge Geometry v2

## Status

Exploratory

## Motivation

Version 1 explored the possibility that knowledge may naturally organize itself into geometric structures. During further exploration, a new question emerged:

> What if the geometry itself changes?

The initial models assume Euclidean geometry.  Pyramids, prisms and dimensions implicitly rely on Euclidean assumptions.

However, organizational knowledge may not behave as a Euclidean space.

## Perspective As A Dimension

Version 1 introduced the possibility that perspective behaves as an additional dimension.

A bounded context may represent a local knowledge space.

An organization may contain multiple knowledge spaces.

Examples:

* Sales
* Operations
* Finance
* Technology

Each perspective observes the same organization. But, each perspective projects different knowledge.

The organization itself may exist as a higher-dimensional structure composed of multiple interconnected perspectives.

## Knowledge Tesseract Hypothesis

If a prism represents a bounded knowledge space and a pyramid represents knowledge refinement, then multiple prisms may coexist inside a larger structure.

Perspective may act as an additional dimension connecting these structures. In this interpretation:

* pyramids explain refinement
* prisms explain boundaries
* perspective explains coexistence

The resulting structure resembles a higher-dimensional knowledge space, like a Tesseract!.

## Beyond Euclidean Knowledge

Current models assume Euclidean geometry. This raises a new question:

> Is knowledge inherently Euclidean?

Possible indicators suggest otherwise.

### Variable Distance

Two concepts may appear close from one perspective and distant from another. Example:

Customer May be central to:

* Sales
* Support

But peripheral to:

* Infrastructure
* Accounting

Distance may depend on perspective.

### Curvature

Some concepts influence multiple knowledge spaces simultaneously. Examples:

* Legal
* Security
* Compliance
* Finance

These concepts appear to affect the shape of surrounding knowledge spaces.

### Non-Linear Navigation

Knowledge discovery rarely follows straight paths. Engineers frequently move through:

* assumptions
* partial understanding
* validation
* correction

Knowledge acquisition may resemble exploration of a curved space rather than movement across a flat one.
