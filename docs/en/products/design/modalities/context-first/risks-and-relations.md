# Risks and tradeoffs

Context-First is useful, but it can fail when applied without discipline. Common risks include:

- endless discovery
- analysis paralysis
- too much documentation before feedback
- unclear stopping criteria
- lack of visible progress
- excessive focus on current state instead of improvement
- modeling the organization instead of solving a business need
- confusing documentation volume with understanding quality

To avoid these risks, the team should keep the iteration progressive. The team should ask:

- Are we learning something that changes the decision?
- Are we reducing relevant uncertainty?
- Are we getting closer to a valuable improvement?
- Are we documenting because it helps, or because it feels safe?
- Do we have enough base to move to the next stage?

Context-First should create clarity. Not become a way to postpone decisions forever.

# Relationship with other modalities

Context-First is not always the best modality. It is best when contextual uncertainty or misunderstanding risks is high.

A team may start with Context-First to understand a broad domain, then move to Problem-First when a valuable improvement becomes clear.

Later, the same team may use Slice-First to validate a small implementation quickly.

The modalities can be combined across iterations, they are not identities. They are strategies.

The right modality depends on the uncertainty, risk, domain maturity, and business urgency of the situation.
